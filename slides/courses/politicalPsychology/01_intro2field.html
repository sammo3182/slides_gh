2.2 Biopolitics

Application of evolutionary psych

  • The biologically adaptive role of various social behaviors, and behavioral genetics
  • Uncovering the heritability of many social and political behaviors
  • Distinction (from rational choice): Grappling increasingly with the benefits of social and political behavior to the collective linked to the controversial theory of group selection

Foucaultâ€™s concept of biopolitics and evolutionary politics in political psychology are related in their focus on the body and population-level dynamics but differ fundamentally in their theoretical foundations, scope, and aims.

Biopolitics, as defined by Foucault, refers to the practices of governance that focus on managing populations through techniques that regulate health, reproduction, and life itself. It is concerned with how political power shapes and controls life and biological processes, often using institutions and state mechanisms (Foucault, 2003). Foucaultâ€™s biopolitics is thus deeply tied to issues of power, knowledge, and the ways in which modern states exert control over citizensâ€™ bodies to create a productive and manageable population.

In contrast, evolutionary politics in political psychology examines how evolutionary mechanisms might shape political behaviors, preferences, and ideologies. This approach is grounded in evolutionary theory and aims to explain how inherited psychological traits and behaviors that were advantageous in ancestral environments influence contemporary political behaviors. Evolutionary political psychology might explore, for example, why certain political attitudes may be adaptive or how in-group/out-group dynamics can be traced to survival advantages in early human societies (Buss, 2005).

While both perspectives can intersectâ€”for instance, both are concerned with health, survival, and the influence of larger systems on individual behaviorâ€”biopolitics is fundamentally about power relations and governance, while evolutionary politics seeks to understand how psychological traits and behaviors have been shaped by evolutionary pressures. Biopolitics often critiques how institutions control biological aspects, whereas evolutionary politics analyzes how inherited traits shape political preferences.

In sum, they are distinct frameworks: biopolitics centers on sociopolitical control and power over life, while evolutionary politics focuses on the biological and psychological bases of political behavior.

2.4 Cognitive psychology

  • How privileged habitual thought work in political decision makings/political cognition
    • Spreading activation: habitual association with stable policy positions
  • Cognitive Economy: cold cognitive biases and hot affective biases
    • Precociousness attitude activation goes against the persuasive political rhetoric
    • Affect-cognition interplay: emotion and elite decision-making
  • Implicit attitudes: political decisions are largely by consciousness while political attitudes can be influenced implicitly
    • IAT measurements

2.5 Intergroup relations

  • Cross-group cognition: Limitation of the cognitive system leads to stereotypes of other groups
  • Group reaction according to the implicit attitudes
    • E.g., members of minority groups react more strongly to negative implicit than explicit attitudes held by a majority group member, underscoring the power of implicit attitudes to shape interpersonal encounters
  • Influence of threat on group behaviors
    • Threat plays a special role in the political life of a collective. It can galvanize and unify an in-group while leading to vilification of an out-group, and is thus particularly potent politically.

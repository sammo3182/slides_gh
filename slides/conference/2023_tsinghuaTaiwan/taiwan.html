Why should we care?

Substantive Importance

  • Leverage of the government (Pan, Wu, and Chang 2017; Liu and Li 2023)
  • Impact across the strait (Wenger and Chen 2017)
  • Signal to the U.S. (Weiss2013?)
  • Pan, Wu, and Chang (2017): Chinese government can take advantage of nationalist protests and influence foreign countries’ belief in China’s resolve in crisis management. The higher nationalism, the more likely Chinese leaders decide to use force.
  • Wenger and Chen (2017): Weak support in the PRC for looser forms of cross-Strait integration.
  • (Weiss2013?): The United States has shown a concern and willingness to accommodate Chinese nationalism when dealing with issues related to Taiwan.

Research Importance

  • Political mobilization (Cheng, Zhang, and Gil De Zúñiga 2022)
  • Group identity → political cohesion (Huddy, Sears, and Levy 2013, chap. 23)
  • Active audience in political communication (Kreiss 2017; Hu and Shao 2022)
  • Cheng, Zhang, and Gil De Zúñiga (2022): identifying and targeting different audiences and adopting differentiated information strategies can effectively mobilize public participation
  • Kreiss (2017): Clarifying the geographical location, demographics, likes and dislikes, and tendencies of the audience can better improve communication efficiency and promote political participation among the public.

Then, Who

  • Known: Hard-core supporters
    • 6% (Pan, Wu, and Chang 2017) vs. 55% (Liu and Li 2023)
    • Ideology-oriented (Pan, Wu, and Chang 2017):
      • Nationalism + peer pressure (Liu and Li 2023)
      • Privileged, strong national pride (Qi, Zhang, and Lin 2023)
    • Decompressor
      • Economic, human, and reputational costs (Liu and Li 2023)
      • Coastal city residents, (Qi, Zhang, and Lin 2023)
  • Unknown: Active watcher & mentality
    • Relying on survey data
      • Cyberspace (Chen et al. 2023, topic focused, though)
    • Missing psychological traits

  • Pan, Wu, and Chang (2017): a telephone survey that covers 2,000 respondents from ten major cities in China

  • Liu and Li (2023): Qualtrics’ online opt-in panel using a quota sampling strategy that targeted pre-specified proportions of gender, age group, and geographic location as benchmarked on the latest census

  • Qi, Zhang, and Lin (2023): nine-city cell phone survey in 2019

  • Liu and Li (2023): armed unification, or ‘wutong’, garners only a slim majority (55%) of support, no more than for a range of less aggressive policy options, from using small-scale warfare, to coercing Taipei into negotiating, to simply maintaining the status quo. Only one out of one hundred rejected all but the most extreme option of ‘wutong’. Analyses of respondent attributes further reveal that aggressive policy preferences are primarily driven by nationalism and peer pressure, but dampened by concerns about the economic, human, and reputational costs of non-peaceful unification and the likelihood of US intervention.

  • Qi, Zhang, and Lin (2023): Wu-tong supporters: the politically, economically, and socially privileged groups and those with stronger national pride and more understanding of Taiwan were more pro-armed unification, while residents of the two coastal cities, Xiamen and Guangzhou, were less supportive.

Exploring Active Taiwan Watchers via Social Media

Data Collection & Research Design

  • Timing: 2016 election (01-14 ~ 01-19)
  • Data
    • Taiwan topic followers via governmental Weibo account
    • Comments and retweets (N = 3,3962) from 191 posts
  • Design
    • Personality analysis (Dinesen, Nørgaard, and Klemmensen 2014; Greene and Robertson 2017; Truex 2022)
    • Language psychology (Pennebaker, Mehl, and Niederhoffer 2003; Pennebaker and King 1999)
      • Explicit traits: Big 5 (openness, conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, neuroticism)
      • Implicit: Depression, intimacy, and veracity

Measurements

  • Psychological traits
    • Explicit: Percentage of feature words from LIWC relates to the traits (Golbeck, Robles, and Turner 2011)
    • Implicit: Function words analysis
      • Pronoun usage and exclusive words (Newman et al. 2003)
  • Predicted variables
    • Retweet behaviors: response rounds, response length, sentence complexity, vocabulary richness, attitudinal preference
    • Communication building: discussion rounds, discussion size, rounds w. substantive meaning, vocabulary richness
  • Alternative explanatory variables: Gender, age, social status, media type, content features (post length, topics, and sentiments)
  • Openness: an interest in culture and curiosity about new experience, e.g., Audit, cash, owe
  • Conscientiousness: individuals’ tendency to pursue order and meet one’s own obligations, e.g., Mate, talk, they,
  • Extraversion: individuals’ tendency to behave and react vigorously in different situations; e.g., Observing, heard, feeling
  • Agreeableness: individuals’ concern for altruism, generosity, and loyalty, e.g., Happy, cried, abandon
  • Neuroticism: the control of impulses and emotions, e.g., Worried, fearful, nervous
  • Depression: I
  • Intimacy: you
  • Honest: we,I, they, he, she , but, except, without

Results

Active Watcher Persona

  • Demographic
    • Not exactly male dominant
    • Not exactly political dominant
    • Not that young
  • Explicit
    • Conscientiousness is the highest, requiring order
    • Agreeableness is the lowest
  • Implicit
    • Intimacy (you) is high, which is fake
    • Veracity is low, lying signal

Event Shock

No big changes

Explanatory power of psychological traits

Personality and behavior

  • Metrics
    • Rounds & length: quantity
    • Complexity & richness: quality
  • Big 5
    • Openness: longer conversation, shorter rounds
    • Conscientiousness: longer but short and simple response
    • Agreeableness: lack → more rounds and rich conversation
    • Neuroticism: reducing quantity and quality but small amount
  • Implicit
    • Intimacy slightly improve the quantity and quality
    • Lying lead to more rounds of sophisticated

Attitudinal Preference

  • Openness showing more salient expression
  • Conscientiousness increases pro-T and anti-M
  • Intimacy improves discussion
  • So does lack of veracity

Personality and communication

  • Extraversion increases the communication
  • Agreelableness reduces it
  • Impact less

Preliminary Conclusions and Future Work

  • Preliminary findings
    • Active Taiwan watchers are not that radical but modest and stable
    • Explicit (openness, conscientiousness,agreeableness) and implicit (veracity) traits both explain watchers’ online behaviors, while the former contributes to the communication construction more.
    • Psychological traits drive social communication in the mainland media to be short-round, relatively simple, and clear-cut.
  • On-going project
    • Vs. nongovernmental accounts
    • Vs. other events
    • Using other measurements

The problem

Data wrangling is a labor

  • A growing volume of research applies the data harmonization process that crunches a large quantity of data from different sources
  • Quality assurance must begin earlier to the data-wrangling step

A high-risk mission

Incomplete/inaccurate data entry

+

Absence of reproducibility

+

Unidentifiable human discretion

  • The conventional approach usually involves a notorious bulk of manual work on indicator identification, data merging, data scaling, and so on
  • Manual wrangling make a full reproducibility of research pipeline more difficulty and undermine the transparency
  • Heterogeneous sources increases the risk
    • older survey files stored in SPSS’s ASCII or portable formats often require extensive restructuring before they can be merged with new format of data.
  • Unfortunately, documentation does not cure everything, esp. the influence of human discretion embedded in manual processing

What would happen if you replicate the data wrangling

146 research teams, same task (Huntington-Klein et al. 2025):

  • Group A (own design + own data): high variation
  • Group B (given design + own data): highest variation
  • Group C (given design + clean data): most consistent

Here we provide a vivid example from real-life research to show how different results the seemingly basic, well-described hand-entered steps can produce. We attempted to replicate the spreadsheets applied in prominent studies following the replication instructions of the publications but with a more automatic process (elaborated below). Points on the diagonal show agreement; points above indicate higher hand-entered values, while points below show lower hand-entered percentages compared to machine-collected data.

For about 85% of country-year-item observations, differences between percentages are negligible-less than half a percent-resulting in points near the plot’s diagonal. However, for the remaining cases, differences are significant and cannot be ignored. These discrepancies stem from decisions on categorization, processing numerators and denominators in ratio calculation, and whether counting respondents excluded from original surveys. Though errors introduced during such processes seems minor and may appear as random noise, they can lead to opposite conclusions (Hu et al. 2025). Such issues pose a major, yet underrecognized, challenge to data harmonization in political science.

Holzmeister et al. (2024) found that researcher-induced variation in estimated effects may even exceed the population variation—and a substantial share stems from data-wrangling decisions.

A solution

A Tao of Data Wrangling

Goal

  1. Manual entry errors (accuracy)
  2. As much data as possible (coverage)
  3. Reproducibility of data wrangling
  1. To incorporate as much available data as possible to provide a base for comparable data and increase generality of the inferences;
  2. To reduce the manual entry errors to improve the accuracy of the harmonized data and analytic data; and
  3. To improve the reproducibility of data wrangling process for the sake of transparency.

Routine

  1. Team-based concept construct and data selection;
  2. Data entry automation;
  3. “Second-order” opening.

Illustration

  • SWIID is a long-running project that seeks to provide harmonized income inequality statistics for the broadest possible coverage of countries and years
  • DCPO is both a method and a database, to address the lack of cross-national and longitudinal data on many topics

Step 1: Team-Based Construct Building and Data Selection

Problem

  • “RA’s pain”
    • Error rates approaching 1% (after the double-check instruction, Barchard and Pace 2011)
    • Example misrouted financial transactions and airline flights (Haegemans et al. 2019)

Solution: Teamwork = Deliberation + Dual-entry

  • Deliberative process: shared construct, region-assigned members, codebook iteration
  • Dual-entry procedure: independent re-coding → compare → flag discrepancies → group discussion
  • SWIID:
    • Told RAs the goal of the research; provided a list of sources mainly from national statistic bureaus to start, but also told them that updated statistics for some countries may come from academic papers, published documents, and other sources—encouraged to add them while recording a valid link.
    • Cross-check with new RAs serves as the dual-entry procedure.
  • DCPO: clearly defining and agreeing upon the latent construct among team members
    • Done by researchers, not RAs.
    • Each team member is assigned survey datasets from specific geographic regions and tasked with identifying potentially relevant items and dimensions based on both general theoretical guidance and region-specific knowledge.
    • Before data selection begins, team members undergo hands-on training on how the method works and what data and metadata (format, weighting variables) to collect—supporting later automation.
    • Dual-entry: each team member reviews and re-codes the survey data originally handled by another member; disputed cases are flagged for group discussion.
  • Teamwork also expands the data pool: both SWIID and DCPO enrolled non-US team members who draw on linguistic and cultural expertise to identify non-English sources and improve precision of data selection.

Step 2: Data Entry Automation

Problem: Entry errors + intransparency

Solution: Automate as much as possible

Method Tools / Example
API 76% of SWIID
Spreadsheet scripted download
Web/PDF scrape tabulapdf, OCR
Hand entry dual-entry cross-check
  • DCPOtools (survey data): converts various file formats to R-readable objects, extracts variables of interest, applies consistent recoding and weighting, outputs structured country-year aggregates
  • tabulapdf: automated PDF table extraction to avoid data-entry errors; OCR extends this to hard-copy sources
  • SWIID: 76% of observations collected via API; scripting covers 90% overall; only 10% requires hand entry—and even then, tabulapdf is used where possible
  • Automation minimizes but does not eliminate errors: OCR may misread digits, software bugs can affect quality; human supervision and spot-checks remain essential

Step 3: “Second-Order” Opening

Problem: Current open-science standards are insufficient

  • “First-order” openness: sharing analysis code + outputs ✓
  • “Second-order” openness: making the DGP transparent ✗

Why it matters: (Huntington-Klein et al. 2025)

  • Given design, own data processing → highest result variation

Solution: Share the full pipeline—collection, wrangling, harmonization

  • “First-order” openness (sharing analysis code/outputs) is now a standard requirement for top journals, but the wrangling stage—where researcher degrees of freedom are largest—remains underdocumented.
  • Huntington-Klein et al. (2025): 146 research teams, same task. Group B (specified design but processed data themselves) produced the highest variation; Group C (given pre-cleaned data) produced the most consistent estimates.
  • If researchers follow Steps 1 and 2, second-order opening becomes both feasible and efficient: simply share the scripts for downloading, formatting, and wrangling.
  • Concrete example: Tai, Hu, and Solt (2024)—readers can trace the DCPO project from start in the GitHub repo, and every data update wave in the corresponding OSF project.

Some discussions

Optimizing data wrangling: An ongoing job

Intelligent agents (LLMs) may soon handle parts of these routines

Researchers’ role

Researchers must steer:

  • Construct development & codebook refinement (iterative deliberation)
  • Human validation of automated outputs (formats, value ranges)
  • Version control and replication materials
  • Computing environment documentation

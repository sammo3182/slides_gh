Class & co-optation

\(H_{2.1}\): ‚Üë \(\text{comparison}_{high\ income}\) ‚Üí perceived inequality ‚Üì ‚Üí redistribution support ‚Üì

\(H_{2.2}\): ‚Üì \(\text{comparison}_{low\ income}\) ‚Üí perceived inequality ‚Üë ‚Üí redistribution support ‚Üë

\(H_{3}\): Public sector employees ‚Üí perceived inequality ‚Üì ‚Üí redistribution support ‚Üì

H1: Social comparisons influence perceptions of inequality and preferences for redistribution. Specifically, individuals who engage in upward (downward) social comparisons, comparing themselves to those who are better (worse) off, are more likely to perceive greater (less) income inequality and express stronger (weaker) support for redistribution policies.*

H2: Income levels significantly affect how individuals are influenced by social comparisons. People with higher incomes tend to experience more positive effects from upward comparisons, receiving optimistic signals that lead them to believe less redistribution is necessary. In contrast, those with lower incomes are more likely to experience negative effects from upward comparisons, feeling a sense of deprivation that increases their demand for greater redistribution.

H3: Public sector employment influences individuals‚Äô welfare and job stability, thereby affecting the intensity of social comparison. Public sector employees, due to their greater job security and benefits, may perceive less risk from social comparisons and, as a result, may be less supportive of redistribution policies compared to non-public sector employees.

According to the Meltzer-Richard Model, is primarily determined by voters‚Äô income levels relative to the median. Individuals with incomes below the median are generally in favor of such policies, as they stand to gain from higher taxes on the wealthy and increased redistribution. They support these measures to improve their own economic conditions. In contrast, those with incomes above the median tend to oppose redistribution policies, as they are concerned that higher taxes and reduced redistribution could diminish their wealth. Therefore, the model suggests that the demand for redistribution is driven by self-interest, with those disadvantaged by income inequality more likely to support policies that aim to address it(Meltzer and Richard 1981).

While wealthier individuals generally show less support for redistribution as their income increases, they are more likely to support such policies in countries with high levels of inequality compared to more equal societies(Dimick, Rueda, and Stegmueller 2017).In more privileged classes, where individuals encounter fewer market-based risks, there is a stronger alignment between values and attitudes towards redistribution. In contrast, among lower classes, immediate material concerns may overshadow value-based considerations unless mitigated by strong redistribution policies(Kulin and Svallfors 2013).

Research has shown that individuals exposed to greater labor market risks are generally more supportive of social insurance programs(Han and Kwon 2020). Occupational risk, including unemployment(Margalit 2013), and uncertainty about future income (Rehm, Hacker, and Schlesinger 2012)also tend to increase support for downward redistribution. For those with lower socioeconomic status, local exposure to inequality tends to increase support for wealth redistribution(Sands 2017). Moreover, both upward and downward social comparisons can be anxiety-inducing, further exacerbating their sense of insecurity(Condon and Wichowsky 2020).

Experimental Design

Pre-registered and highly inspired by Condon and Wichowsky (2020) üòò

[In Chinese] Please imagine the individuals in Chinese society who are the (worst/best) off‚Äîthose who have the (least/most) wealth, (lowest/highest) education level, and (least respected/most respected) jobs. Specifically, consider how you differ from these individuals in terms of your own income, educational background, and job status.

Participants in the control group will be asked to imagine conversing with individuals who have similar income, education levels, and social status as themselves, and to reflect on what topics might arise in such a conversation.

The issue of inequality has become a pressing social problem that needs to be addressed urgently.

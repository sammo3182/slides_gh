Learned and Puzzled in Identity Research

Known

  • Identity matters in general (Bernstein 2005; Peters and Arnesen 2017; Li 2020)
  • On important decisions (e.g., judicial ~)
    • Judges (vs. accused):
      • Race: Matters (Steffensmeier and Britt 2001) vs. not matters (Anwar, Bayer, and Hjalmarsson 2012)
      • Gender: Matters in gender-related effects (Boyd, Epstein, and Martin 2010) vs. only gender-related (Haire and Moyer 2015)
      • Party: Democratics vs. Republican (Ulmer 1962; Epstein et al. 2007)
    • Jurors: Race and gender(DeCamp and DeCamp 2020; Hoekstra and Street 2021) matters

  • Race

    • Steffensmeier and Britt found that black judges were slightly more likely to sentence defendants to prison, regardless of the defendants‚Äô race (Steffensmeier and Britt 2001). Cohen and Yang found that black judges issue shorten sentences than others in federal district courts.

  • In cases where race or ethnicity is salient, such as those of affirmative action, racial discrimination, and racial harassment, researchers found that black judges tended to vote in favor of nonwhite plaintiffs (Cohen and Yang 2019).

  • Anwar, however, found no apparent sentencing disparities in felony cases based on judges‚Äô race (Anwar, Bayer, and Hjalmarsson 2012).

  • Gender

    • Early studies found that female judges ruled more liberally than males in gender-related cases, such as sex discrimination ones (Gryski, Main, and Dixon 1986; Allen and Wall 1993).
    • Boyd and Epstein examined the decisions of federal appellate court judges in 13 areas and found consistent gender effects only in cases of sex discrimination (Boyd, Epstein, and Martin 2010). There even exists a ‚Äúdaughter effect.‚Äù Glynn et al. collected household data on 224 judges in the US courts of Appeals and nearly 10,000 gender-related cases decided by those judges. They found that judges with daughters become more Democratic in these cases. Having a daughter increased judges‚Äô knowledge on issues regarding sexual harassment claims.
    • Other scholars nonetheless reported mixed findings (Glynn and Sen 2015). Ashenfelter et al. and Haire et al. reported no or only a slight relationship between gender and judicial decisions (Ashenfelter, Eisenberg, and Schwab 1995; Haire and Moyer 2015). Gill et al. found that when hearing immigration appeals, all-male appellate panels were much harsher with male litigants than female litigants (Gill, Kagan, and Marouf 2017).

  • Party identity

    • Ulmer found that Michigan Supreme Court Justices elected under a Democratic Party label were more likely to have pro-worker leanings (Ulmer 1962). The ensuing attitudinal model postulates a strong relationship between ideology and judicial decision-making (Segal and Spaeth 1993, 2002; Martin and Quinn 2002). Similarly, recent research on state courts primarily uses party labels as a proxy of state judges‚Äô ideological leaning and lends strong evidence to the attitudinal model (Giles, Hettinger, and Peppers 2001; Epstein et al. 2007).

Results

\(H_1a\): assessors‚Äô official identity, rather than other identities, are more likely to associate with a court decision in favor of the government.
\(H_1b\): assessors‚Äô embedded official identity (leading cadres) are more salient than the detached identity(people‚Äôs representatives).

Amplifier Effect

No: \(H_2a\): The official assessors‚Äô influence on the court decisions is larger when they are outnumbered.
Yes \(H_2b\): Assessors‚Äô detached identity (people‚Äôs representatives) have equal influence as the embedded official identity (leading cadres) when they are amplified (by being outnumbered).
